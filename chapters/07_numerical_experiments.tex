\chapter{Numerical experiments}

This chapter presents the experimental setup and results for each of the three studies comprising this thesis. For each study, we describe the motivation, experimental configuration, main results, and their interpretation.

\section{Study 1: Uncertainty Estimation via Token-Wise Entropy}

The gap between a model's apparent confidence and its actual correctness poses critical risks in high-stakes applications. In domains such as healthcare, law, education, and economics, an LLM's overestimation of its own accuracy could result in harmful decisions or misinformation. Existing uncertainty estimation methods tend to evaluate how good an overall confidence estimate is, but often ignore why uncertainty appeared in a specific case and how it relates to the nature of the question being asked. Furthermore, many existing methods generalize poorly for concise multiple-choice questions where the model's response consists of only one or two tokens. This study investigates whether the entropy of the model's output distribution over the answer token can serve as a reliable indicator of answer correctness, and how this signal varies across question topics and reasoning requirements.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We use the MMLU-Pro benchmark~\cite{wang2024mmlu}, which contains approximately 12,000 challenging multiple-choice questions with 10 answer choices per question across 14 diverse domains including Biology, Chemistry, Computer Science, Economics, Engineering, Health, History, Law, Math, Philosophy, Physics, Psychology, Business, and Other. The dataset was chosen for its increased difficulty compared to the original MMLU and its improved robustness against data leakage, as official reports for Phi-4 and Mistral-Small-24B also used this extended dataset.

We evaluate four decoder-only large language models spanning a wide range of parameter counts: Phi-4~\cite{abdin2024phi}, Mistral-Small-24B-Base-2501~\cite{jiang2023mistral}, Qwen-2.5-1.5B, and Qwen-2.5-72B~\cite{qwen2024}. This selection allows us to examine how model scale affects the relationship between entropy and answer correctness. For each model, we prompt it to answer questions directly (without chain-of-thought reasoning) and compute the Shannon entropy of the output probability distribution over the answer token. We measure the predictive power of this entropy signal using ROC-AUC for distinguishing correct from incorrect answers, stratified by subject domain and reasoning requirement.

The results reveal several important patterns. Entropy is an effective predictor of answer correctness in knowledge-dependent domains, with Biology achieving the highest ROC-AUC of 0.83 for Qwen-72B, followed by Economics (0.80) and Computer Science (0.78). In contrast, performance is notably lower for reasoning-intensive domains: Math achieves only 0.55 ROC-AUC for Phi-4 and Law only 0.50. This domain dependence suggests that entropy primarily captures \textit{knowledge uncertainty}---whether the model has encountered similar factual content during training---rather than \textit{reasoning difficulty}.

The effect of model scale is substantial and consistent. Qwen-72B achieves an overall ROC-AUC of 0.77, compared to 0.70 for Qwen-1.5B (same architecture family but 48$\times$ fewer parameters), 0.58 for Phi-4, and 0.52 for Mistral. This scaling trend holds across all domains without exception, suggesting that larger models develop more informative entropy signals.

The reasoning analysis further supports the knowledge-versus-reasoning interpretation. For Qwen-72B, questions that do not require reasoning achieve ROC-AUC of 0.79, compared to 0.73 for questions requiring high reasoning. This gap is even more pronounced for Mistral (0.59 vs. 0.53). Low entropy is thus a marginally better predictor of correctness for factual recall questions than for multi-step reasoning problems.

The Model-as-Judge (MASJ) approach, in which a large auxiliary LLM estimates question difficulty, performs near-randomly with ROC-AUC values around 0.49--0.57 across all models. Both the numerical and nominal MASJ scores relate little to actual question complexity, indicating that MASJ captures different aspects of a question's properties that are largely irrelevant to predicting model correctness.

Mistral exhibits unstable calibration dynamics, characterized by erratic accuracy fluctuations in low-confidence regions. Interestingly, Qwen-1.5B emerges as an outlier, surpassing its larger counterpart in calibration quality: its confidence-accuracy relationship progresses monotonically across entropy bins with minimal extreme deviations. These results underscore that systematic overconfidence is a pervasive issue across model architectures, particularly in high-certainty regimes where all models significantly overestimate their reliability.

\subsection{Discussion}

This study demonstrates that single-token answer entropy is a practical and effective signal for predicting LLM answer correctness, but with important caveats. The signal is most informative for knowledge-dependent domains where the model either knows the answer (low entropy) or does not (high entropy). For questions requiring multi-step reasoning, the entropy of the final answer token is less informative because the model may be uncertain about the reasoning process while still arriving at the correct answer, or conversely, may be confident in an incorrect reasoning chain.

The observation that MASJ performs near-randomly is significant: it suggests that external judges, even when using large auxiliary models, capture fundamentally different properties than the model's own internal uncertainty. This finding motivates the use of intrinsic signals (output distributions) rather than external evaluation for uncertainty estimation.

We also note that the MMLU-Pro dataset exhibits internal biases in question complexity. The distribution of reasoning requirements varies significantly by topic---engineering questions require reasoning in approximately 90\% of cases, while philosophy requires it in only about 50\%. This suggests that more balanced and less biased datasets would be needed for fair measurement of LLM progress across domains.

\section{Study 2: Language Steering in Latent Space}

Multilingual large language models frequently exhibit unintended code-switching---generating tokens in languages other than the target despite explicit monolingual instructions. In deployed systems, such behavior degrades user trust and experience. For instance, a customer-service chatbot prompted in Spanish may switch to English mid-response, confusing users. Existing solutions require costly fine-tuning on monolingual data, reinforcement learning with language-specific rewards, or brittle prompt engineering. This study investigates whether the geometric structure of multilingual representations in the model's latent space can be exploited for a cheaper alternative: inference-time activation steering that requires no additional training.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate two multilingual models of different architectures: Qwen2.5-1.5B with 28 layers and Llama-3.2-1B with 16 layers. We test four language pairs spanning different typological distances: English--Chinese (distant, different script), English--Spanish (close, same script), English--Russian (moderate, different script), and English--Hindi (distant, different script). This selection allows us to examine how typological similarity and script differences affect steering effectiveness.

For PCA fitting, we use the Flores Plus dataset~\cite{nllb2024} with 50 parallel samples per language and 100 samples for validation. The parallel nature of the corpus is essential: because the semantic content is held constant across languages, language identity becomes the dominant variance source in the hidden state space. For steering evaluation, we use 2,467 TED Talks samples with artificial code-switching, where each sample is truncated to 20 words, split at the midpoint, and the second half is translated to the target language using Gemini 2.5 Flash, producing texts that begin in English and switch mid-sentence.

\subsection{Discussion}

The generation-based evaluation confirms that distributional optimization transfers well to actual text generation, but also reveals important fragility. Steering coefficients are highly sensitive to magnitude: small changes can shift the model from fluent code-switched output to degenerate repetition. The CSI-optimized coefficients cluster near the maximum tested value ($-5.0$), suggesting that direct optimization of generation metrics pushes toward an aggressive regime where language control and fluency trade off sharply. Qualitative inspection of generated text confirms this: for English--Spanish, steering successfully redirects generation from Spanish tokens to coherent English, but for English--Russian with aggressive coefficients, steering eliminates Russian but produces repetitive output patterns (e.g., ``first year of the first year of the first year...'').

This fluency--control trade-off represents a fundamental challenge for practical deployment. While the method achieves strong language consistency, the optimal operating point requires careful calibration per language pair, and no principled method for automatic coefficient selection has been developed. Combining steering with constrained decoding or adaptive coefficient scheduling represents a promising direction for addressing this limitation.

\section{Study 3: Complexity-Aware Fine-Tuning}

General-purpose LLMs excel across diverse tasks, but deploying them often requires domain-specific fine-tuning. A standard approach is to train on all available in-domain data with uniform supervised fine-tuning (SFT). However, recent work has concentrated primarily on optimizing training mechanics---objectives, schedulers, and regularizers---while comparatively little attention has been paid to data curation. Curriculum-style approaches have been explored with increasing complexity of data during training, but with limited gains in final accuracy and efficiency, suggesting that more principled strategies are needed. This study investigates whether entropy-based complexity estimation can guide a more efficient fine-tuning pipeline that applies different training strategies to examples of different difficulty.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We conduct experiments across three datasets that cover different domains and question types. MMLU-Pro provides approximately 12,000 multiple-choice questions across 14 domains with varying complexity. GSM8K~\cite{cobbe2021gsm8k} contains 8,500 grade school math word problems requiring multi-step arithmetic reasoning. MedMCQA~\cite{pmlr-v174-pal22a} offers large-scale medical entrance exam questions covering 2,400 healthcare topics across 21 medical subjects.

For student models, we use three architectures: Qwen2.5-3B, Phi-4-Mini, and LLama-3.2-3B. For chain-of-thought distillation on hard examples, we use an ensemble of teacher models: DeepSeek-V3-0324, Qwen-3-235B, and Llama-4-Maverick. The data is randomly split into train, validation, and test sets with a 70:10:20 ratio, and balanced so that the number of entries in each complexity group is equal.

We compare our pipeline against four baselines. Standard SFT trains on all data without any complexity split. Curriculum-based SFT applies progressive difficulty ordering (3 epochs on easy data, 3 on medium, 4 on hard). Full distillation applies chain-of-thought distillation to all examples regardless of difficulty. The alternative pipeline reverses our approach: SFT on hard data first, then CoT distillation on easy and medium data.

This suggests that the complexity-aware approach makes better use of training data by avoiding redundant computation on examples the model can already handle.

\subsection{Sensitivity Study}

\subsubsection{Complexity Metric Selection}

We compare single-token answer entropy against alternative complexity metrics: MASJ reasoning score, MASJ education level, cross-entropy, and various chain-of-thought entropy aggregations. Single-token entropy yields the best ROC-AUC for complexity estimation (0.72 for Qwen-3B without domain split) and provides the clearest separation between easy and hard examples during SFT training. MASJ-based metrics achieve ROC-AUC around 0.50--0.57, barely above random, making them unsuitable for data stratification. Chain-of-thought entropy variants provide higher answer accuracy but lower ROC-AUC scores, making them less suitable for complexity estimation. Maximum-based measures (CoT Max, Sequence Mean Max) consistently outperform mean-based approaches, suggesting that peak uncertainty moments better indicate question difficulty than average uncertainty.

We also evaluate cross-entropy as an alternative to entropy for the pipeline's complexity metric. Figure~\ref{fig:entropy_vs_crossentropy} shows that entropy and cross-entropy achieve comparable pipeline performance for Qwen-3B, while Phi-4-Mini demonstrates superior performance with entropy. The difference can be attributed to entropy capturing information across the entire output distribution rather than focusing only on the correct token.

\subsubsection{Curriculum Learning Fragility}

Standard curriculum learning, which applies SFT with progressively increasing difficulty, shows extreme sensitivity to hyperparameters. Figure~\ref{fig:curriculum_fragility} illustrates this fragility: with 10 epochs of training, curriculum-based SFT achieves moderate performance, but extending to 20 epochs causes Qwen-3B to plateau at a lower accuracy while Phi-4-Mini quickly overfits and its performance degrades. This demonstrates that naive complexity-aware ordering without differentiated training strategies---using only SFT throughout---is insufficient. The key insight of our approach is that different complexity levels require fundamentally different training interventions, not merely different ordering.

\subsection{Discussion}

The results confirm that entropy works as a reliable, automatic proxy for question difficulty. The pipeline achieves accuracies of 0.52 and 0.64 compared to 0.39 and 0.51 for baseline SFT on Qwen-3B and Phi-4-Mini respectively, while using 81\% less data than full distillation. This finding suggests that curriculum ideas remain relevant for modern LLMs: letting the model focus on what it can already solve directly, while providing extra reasoning guidance only where it struggles, yields a better allocation of limited model capacity.

The pipeline is fully automated and can be integrated into existing fine-tuning workflows. All that is needed is a single forward pass through the student model to compute entropy scores, followed by sorting and partitioning the data. The distillation step for hard examples requires teacher model inference, but this applies to only approximately 25\% of the training data, substantially reducing the cost compared to full distillation.

However, several limitations should be noted. Low entropy can still correspond to hallucinations, leading to imperfect complexity identification. The three-way equal split was chosen for simplicity but may not be optimal for all datasets. In low-resource settings where a teacher model is unavailable or imperfect, the benefit of chain-of-thought distillation is reduced. Finally, the approach has been validated only on question-answering tasks with clear correct answers; its applicability to open-ended generation remains to be investigated.

\section{Summary of Results}

Across all three studies, we demonstrate that signals from the model's forward pass provide valuable and complementary information for addressing key challenges in LLM deployment. Token-wise entropy achieves ROC-AUC up to 0.83 for predicting answer correctness in knowledge-dependent domains, with the signal quality improving with model scale, while the Model-as-Judge approach performs near-randomly. PCA-identified language directions enable 25--55\% reduction in distributional divergence and 63--95\% reduction in Code-Switching Index in generated text across four language pairs, using only simple linear projections applied to the final layers. Complexity-aware fine-tuning matches or outperforms full distillation while using 79--82\% less training data, generalizing across three datasets and three model architectures, and demonstrating that automatic complexity estimation via entropy enables intelligent allocation of training resources.

These results validate our central hypothesis that accessible signals from forward passes---both output distributions and internal representations---encode sufficient information to improve reliability, control, and efficiency without requiring architectural modifications. The three studies are unified by the principle that a single forward pass contains rich information about the model's confidence, the input's characteristics, and the relationship between the two.
